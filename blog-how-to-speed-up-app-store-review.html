1. Use Apple's Expedited Review Process (Fastest Method)

Apple has an official expedited review pathway for apps with urgent situations. When used correctly, it delivers decisions in 24–48 hours.

Eligible reasons include:

  • Critical bug fixes — a bug causing crashes or data loss for users
  • Time-sensitive events — a product launch, announcement, or promotion with a hard deadline
  • Security vulnerabilities — urgent security patches
  • Government app requirements — apps needed for legal or regulatory compliance

The problem: Most developers who apply for expedited review get rejected. Why? They submit requests that are too vague, don't follow Apple's required format, or aren't compelling enough to meet the bar.

The expedited request needs to be:

  • Specific about the problem (not "we want faster review")
  • Evidence-backed with clear business or user impact
  • Formatted exactly as Apple requires
  • Submitted through the correct channel

When done right, approval rates are high. When done wrong, you've wasted days.

2. Fix Rejection Issues Before They Happen

Rejections add 3–7 days minimum. The fix is obvious but many developers skip it: read Apple's rejection reasons carefully and fix them completely before resubmitting.

Most common rejection reasons in 2026:

  • Guideline 2.1 — App completeness — missing account info, demo credentials, or incomplete functionality in the build you submitted
  • Guideline 4.2.3 — App Store Experience — UI that feels like a web wrapper instead of a native app
  • Privacy policy issues — missing or inadequate privacy policy URL
  • In-app purchase issues — incorrect implementation of Apple's payment system

Before every submission, run through Apple's review guidelines with fresh eyes. Have someone unfamiliar with your app read the notes and confirm you've addressed every point.

3. Optimize Your App Metadata

Apple's review team looks at more than your app binary. Incomplete or unclear metadata is a common reason for delay or rejection.

Double-check before submitting:

  • App name and subtitle are clear and not misleading
  • Category selections are accurate and primary
  • Screenshots match the current version of your app
  • Description doesn't make claims your app can't support
  • Privacy policy URL is live and the policy is comprehensive
  • App Preview videos (if used) show real device footage

4. Submit a Truly Complete App

One of the fastest ways to get rejected quickly is to submit an app that's missing core functionality. Apple is strict about Guideline 2.1 (App Completeness).

Before you click Submit for Review:

  • Every button works and does something real
  • All account creation / sign-in flows are functional
  • You've tested on the exact device types and OS versions you've declared
  • All required fields in App Store Connect are filled in
  • Test credentials are provided if your app has a login wall

5. Follow Apple's Design Guidelines Exactly

Apps that look unfinished or violate Apple's design guidelines get stuck in review longer. Apple's reviewers spend less time on apps that clearly comply.

Key things to get right:

  • Don't hide system share sheets or chrome — let users navigate iOS naturally
  • Use system fonts or properly licensed custom fonts
  • Respect safe area insets on all device sizes
  • No placeholder content or "lorem ipsum" anywhere
  • Dark mode support where appropriate

6. Time Your Submission Strategically

Apple's review team has a backlog that varies by day and season. Review times are typically longer on Mondays and during major Apple event windows (WWDC, iPhone launch windows). They're typically shorter mid-week.

If your launch isn't time-sensitive, submitting Tuesday–Thursday may get you a faster initial review than a Monday submission.

Also: new app submissions sometimes process faster than app updates. If you're doing a significant redesign, sometimes a fresh submission (as a new app) gets reviewed more quickly than an update to an existing app.

7. Follow Up the Right Way

If your app has been in review for more than 3 days (unusual in 2026), you can follow up — but do it correctly.

What works:

  • Use the App Store Connect contact form — select "App Review" and be specific and polite
  • Reference your app's Bundle ID and the date submitted
  • Explain any time-sensitive context clearly (launch event, critical bug, etc.)

What doesn't work:

  • Emailing Apple reviewers directly — they can't receive external email
  • Resubmitting the same binary — just adds another review to the queue
  • Being aggressive or demanding — this will not speed up your review
